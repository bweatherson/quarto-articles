## Fictional Anti-Realism {#sec-fiction}

I've suggested that what's true in a story is what's true in a Humberstone possibility—a potentially incomplete entity that may leave certain facts undetermined. This section defends an anti-realist position about fictional truth against the realism of Blumberg & Holguín, while accepting their key criticisms of Lewis's approach. The core claim is not merely that we lack knowledge about Holmes's cousin count, but that there is no fact of the matter at all. This has the virtue of explaining both the accuracy-dominance phenomena discussed in Section 2 and the intuitions that motivate the "wonder cases" that trouble Lewis's theory.

Lewis and Blumberg & Holguín agree on one crucial point: fictional claims involve an implicit modal operator. When we say "Holmes is a detective," we mean something more like "necessarily in the story, Holmes is a detective." They disagree about the structure of this operator. Lewis treats it as a standard necessity operator □, which commutes with disjunction: □(p ∨ q) can be true when □p and □q are both false. Blumberg & Holguín reject this, arguing for "scopelessness"—the principle that the fiction operator commutes with negation itself, which entails that the fiction picks out a unique world in which every proposition is either true or false.^[More formally, their key principle is that ¬□_f p iff □_f ¬p. This is much stronger than Lewis's modal operator and forces a commitment to uniqueness and excluded middle within the fiction.]

The wonder cases provide powerful motivation for Blumberg & Holguín's position. It is genuinely felicitous to ask "I wonder whether Holmes has an odd number of cousins" or to wonder about the color of Bud's coin in JAG. Under Lewis's analysis, I would know the answer: I would know that neither the definite claim nor its negation is entailed by the fiction, which is precisely a failure of the fiction to determine the matter. Yet the felicity of the wonder case seems to require genuine indeterminacy from my perspective—genuine uncertainty about how things stand in the story. Lewis's operator, making every proposition either necessarily true or necessarily false in the fiction, seems to eliminate this possibility.

But here's where I diverge from Blumberg & Holguín. Their conclusion—that realism is true, that the fiction does pick out a unique world with a determinate answer for every proposition—strikes me as incredible. Not simply unknowable, but literally inconsistent with how we think about and create fiction. The appeal to intuition here is powerful, especially when we consider fictional works with less determinate structure than prose fiction.

Consider a song that tells a simple story. Perhaps a folk ballad where we learn that a character once walked down a road and encountered a stranger. The song never specifies what color the stranger's coat was. When fans argue about it—"I always imagined a blue coat," "No, clearly it was grey"—we don't think either fan has discovered a hidden truth about the song's world. We think each is imposing a further specification on an inherently underspecified fictional scenario. The folk ballad doesn't determine the coat's color, and there is no fact of the matter, not because we lack access to the truth but because the fiction simply doesn't settle it.^[This intuition extends across many forms of fiction. In fan communities, the practice of "fanfiction" itself presupposes that the original work leaves gaps. A fan writing a prequel or exploring an untold episode of a character's life isn't describing what was always true in the story's world—they're extending and completing the story in a particular direction.]

The possibility of consistent but incompatible extensions of the same fiction makes this intuition even sharper. Suppose two fans each write a detailed, internally consistent fanfiction that extends the Holmes canon beyond Conan Doyle's explicit stories. The first fan writes a genealogy establishing that Holmes has exactly 47 cousins; the second establishes that he has 52 cousins. Both completions are perfectly coherent. Both are faithful to the original stories. Yet they cannot both be true of the same world. If we must say that exactly one of them captures what the fiction really determines, we face an odd question: on what principled grounds? The original Conan Doyle stories don't privilege one answer over the other. There's no hidden fact about which genealogy is "correct." Each fan has simply made a choice about how to complete an incompletely specified narrative.

This is where Blumberg & Holguín's theory reaches an impasse. They're committed to the claim that the fiction does determine a unique world—that despite appearances, there really is a fact of the matter about Holmes's cousins, about the stranger's coat in the folk song, about which fanfiction "got it right." The unknowability is epistemological, not metaphysical; the fiction determines the truth, we just can't know what it is. But this seems to multiply mysterious facts beyond necessity. It requires that every conceivable question about the fictional world has an answer, grounded in some non-obvious sense in which the fiction "really" determines that answer, even when no explicit story does and multiple consistent completions exist.

My proposal is simpler. I'll argue that what's true in a story is what's true in a possibility—in the Humberstone sense developed in Section 1. A possibility is not a possible world in the classical sense; it need not be complete. It can leave certain facts undetermined. When we assert that something is true in a story, we assert that it's true in some (or perhaps all, or perhaps the most relevant) possibilities compatible with the story. When a story fails to determine a fact, there are multiple compatible possibilities—one where Holmes has 47 cousins, one where he has 52, and indefinitely many others. There is no fact of the matter because the fiction doesn't single out a unique world; it's compatible with multiple incompletely specified scenarios.

This anti-realist position preserves everything we wanted from Lewis's account: it explains how □(p ∨ q) can be true while □p and □q are both false, because disjunction can be true in a Humberstone possibility without either disjunct being true. It explains how we can genuinely wonder about whether Holmes has an odd or even number of cousins—not because we're ignorant of a hidden truth, but because the question is genuinely undetermined, and our uncertainty maps onto genuine indeterminacy in the fiction itself. And it avoids the uncomfortable conclusion that countless facts about fictional worlds are determined but unknowable.

It also connects naturally to the Dempster-Shafer framework that structures this paper. If what's true in fiction is what's true in a possibility, and possibilities can be incomplete, then to give a complete account of fictional truth, we need a framework that handles both classical truth values and gaps. The Dempster-Shafer approach, which I'll elaborate in subsequent sections, provides such a framework. For now, the key point is this: fictional anti-realism, combined with possibility semantics, gives us a coherent account that respects both the intuitive indeterminacy of fictional worlds and the logical structure of how fictional truths behave under modal operators.
