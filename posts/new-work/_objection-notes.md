# Objections in Section 3


## Bivalence Objection

Blumberg and Holguin's (25) example

1. You can't really say that: literally can't express it in the language.
2. Even if you could, you couldn't know it
3. Couldn't know it follows from Humberstone principles. Seems over the top, but the universal acceptability of "wondering whether" locutions suggests it might be right.


## Expressive Power Objection

Odd that the language doesn't let us state what we want to say

1. We live with this in Tarski-style theories of truth
2. We can say some extra things in the special case of talking about actuality by an *ACTUALLY* operator
3. This behaves weirdly; doesn't have Ap <-> p as a priori like in Davies & Humberstone. But it lets us say truly there is no fact of the matter about Holmes's cousins (even if we can't quite know that). If p is Holmes has even # of cousins, then ~Ap and ~A~p are both extremely probable.

## Disjunction is weird

1. At this point it would be better to go non-classical than accept fictional reality. Intuitionist or Fine-style incomplete systems would be better.
2. But why should we think disjunction is simple. Everyone who looks at it comes away thinking it is very hard. Jennings, Humberstone, Fusco
