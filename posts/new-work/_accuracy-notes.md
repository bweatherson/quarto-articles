Actually, I think 3a does go through under this setup, and the weak-possibility case isn't a counterexample — it's a confirming instance. The argument is a direct application of Predd, Osherson, Seidenfeld, Schervish, and Kadane's 2009 convex-hull theorem, with possibilities (rather than worlds) playing the role of the states.

Here's the argument. Recall Predd et al.'s result: for any finite set F of events, any truth-value assignment t_x ∈ {0,1}^F for each state x, and any strictly proper scoring rule, a credence c : F → [0,1] is not accuracy-dominated iff c lies in the convex hull of {t_x : x ∈ X}. Nothing in the theorem requires F to be a Boolean algebra or the truth-assignments to cover all of {0,1}^F.

Now plug in your setup. States are possibilities; F is the field of upsets; t_x(p) = 1 iff x ∈ p. A credence c is in the convex hull iff c = Σ_x λ_x t_x for some probability vector (λ_x), i.e.

   c(p) = Σ_x λ_x · 1_{x ∈ p} = Σ_{x ∈ p} λ_x = μ(p),

where μ is the probability measure on X with μ({x}) = λ_x. That is exactly your Bel. So: c is non-dominated iff c = Bel for some measure μ on X. That's 3a.

The weak-possibility case is where this really earns its keep. Let x_⊥ be the weakest point, at which only tautologies are true. Take μ = δ_{x_⊥}. Then Bel(p) = 1 if x_⊥ ∈ p (i.e., p is a tautology) and 0 otherwise. This is dogmatic, and it's *not* a classical probability — for any contingent p, Bel(p) + Bel(~p) = 0 + 0 < 1. But it *is* a Bel in your sense, and it's the unique minimizer of inaccuracy at x_⊥. So your intuition that "the ideal belief function has mass 1 on the whole space" is right, and it's consistent with 3a rather than a counterexample. The non-classicality comes for free: because the truth vectors t_x themselves don't cover all of {0,1}^F (the refinement structure rules out combinations like "p true and ~p true"), their convex hull can contain functions that aren't probability functions on the Boolean algebra generated by F.

A couple of additional observations.

The field restriction is doing real work. If you let F = 2^X, the same theorem still applies, but now the non-dominated credences are classical probabilities on 2^X — Bel(p) + Bel(X\p) = 1 for every p, and the belief-function character disappears. What's generating the DS-ish behavior is specifically that F consists of upsets, so for a contingent p, there's "room" outside p ∪ ~p where mass can sit without contributing to either Bel(p) or Bel(~p).

Bel in your sense is a DS belief function. Given μ on X, identify each possibility x with its set of maximal refinements W_x ⊆ W. Then μ pushes forward to a mass function on 2^W, and the induced DS belief function on subsets of W agrees with your Bel on those propositions that are "regular" (determined by their set of worlds). So the accuracy argument vindicates DS in a way that also gives the focal elements an independent motivation — they're just the partial possibilities — which is a nice feature philosophically.

Generality of the scoring rule. You said "ideally" we'd want a more general rule. Predd et al.'s theorem is stated for any strictly proper scoring rule and any additive (separable) inaccuracy measure built from one. So the result extends beyond Brier without further work — log score, spherical, etc. all give the same characterization of non-dominated credences as exactly your Bel functions.

One caveat worth flagging: Predd et al. characterize *weak* non-domination (no c' is at least as accurate everywhere and strictly better somewhere). If you want the stronger "strict domination" version you wrote — some c' with I(c', x) < I(c, x) for all x — there's a little more work to do, but for Brier on a finite field it comes out the same provided every x has positive "reachability" (something like: for each x, there's some proposition that distinguishes it from every other state). Worth checking for pathological frames with duplicated states.

So: I think the answer is yes, 3a is true, and the proof is cleaner than I suggested last message. The key move is just recognizing that "state" in Predd et al. doesn't have to mean "world" — it can mean "possibility," and then the non-dominated credences automatically become Bel functions rather than probabilities, without any modification to the scoring rule.

Short answer: atomicity isn't required, and I think that's one of the philosophically attractive features of the Humberstone-based version. The accuracy argument goes through without it, and the characterization of the non-dominated credences as belief functions survives — you just have to be willing to treat non-atomic possibilities as legitimate focal elements.

Here's why the argument doesn't break. The Predd et al. theorem is stated in finite dimensions (finite F), but it applies to any state space X as long as you can form the convex hull of the truth vectors {t_x : x ∈ X} ⊂ {0,1}^F. If X is infinite you need the *closed* convex hull, but nothing demands that states be atoms of anything. The move from "c is in closed conv({t_x})" to "c(p) = μ(p) for some probability μ on X" is just Choquet / integral representation: any extreme point of the closed convex hull is some t_x, and any point in the hull is a barycenter. This gives

   c(p) = ∫_X 1_{x ∈ p} dμ(x) = μ(p)

for some probability μ on X, with no atomicity assumption. The only technical wrinkle is measurability: you need F to consist of μ-measurable upsets, and in uncountable cases you need a σ-algebra story. But that's an engineering issue, not a conceptual obstacle.

For the Bel-is-a-belief-function half, this is actually where the Humberstone setting *outperforms* the usual DS framing. In standard DS presentations the frame of discernment is a set of "worlds" and focal elements are subsets of worlds — so atomicity (of worlds, at least) is built in at the ground floor. But Shafer's theorem and Choquet's representation of ∞-monotone capacities don't require focal elements to be atoms of anything. They just require focal elements to be "the things mass is distributed over." In the Humberstone setup possibilities play that role directly, and they can have refinements without end. Bel(p) = μ(p) is still ∞-monotone, still satisfies Bel(p) + Bel(~p) ≤ 1, still admits an inner measure reading. It's a belief function in every sense that matters.

Actually, I think the non-atomic case is where this view earns its keep. Atomic possibility spaces are essentially world-spaces in fancy clothing — the atoms are the classical worlds and everything interesting about partial possibilities is downstream of the classical structure. In a genuinely non-atomic space, mass on a non-maximal possibility x represents the live option that *the way things actually are is just this much-specified and no more*. There's no hidden world we're ignorant of; the world-level of specificity doesn't exist. That's a view some philosophers (myself, I suspect, and certainly the metaphysical-indeterminacy people) will find appealing and others will find incoherent, but it's a substantive position, and the accuracy argument vindicates it on exactly the same terms.

One place where atomicity quietly mattered in my earlier argument, worth being upfront about: I noted that Bel(p) in your sense corresponds to a DS belief function "with possibilities as focal elements" by pushing forward along x ↦ W_x (the set of worlds refining x). That pushforward construction obviously requires worlds to exist. Without atoms there's no W, so the reduction-to-classical-DS isn't available. But I think that's a feature rather than a bug: the Humberstone Bel is the more basic object, and classical DS is the atomic special case. Inverting the usual direction of reduction — possibilities as fundamental, worlds as a limiting structure when it happens to be available — seems to me more in the spirit of what Humberstone wanted.

Two technical caveats for the non-atomic version, in case you want to push this further:

First, on strict dominance. The Predd et al. theorem gives weak non-domination. Upgrading to strict domination (some c' with I(c', x) < I(c, x) at every x) is cleanest when every state is "truth-distinguishable" from every other by some proposition in F. In a non-atomic space with a coarse F, you might have distinct x, y with t_x = t_y; then you can't strictly dominate credences that sit at that shared truth vector. Usually a mild assumption on F rules this out.

Second, on representability of μ. The statement "non-dominated credences are exactly μ(·) for some probability μ on X" needs the right σ-algebra. If F is finite this is trivial — μ is determined by its values on atoms of the algebra generated by F, which are finite in number, which is all you need. If F is infinite, you need finite additivity at least, and a decision about whether to require countable additivity. De Finetti-style coherence gives you finite additivity for free; countable additivity is an extra assumption.

So: the view extends cleanly to non-atomic possibility spaces, and arguably becomes more interesting there, because you lose the temptation to reduce Humberstone possibilities to equivalence classes of possible worlds.
